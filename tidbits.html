Chris' Tidbits

Reflections on growing up in Southampton

As a second-grader in Mrs. Malanson's class, how exciting it was to walk from the Larrabee school to the now Old Edwards Library--a long walk and the library was so big!


Math was not my favorite subject in grade school, partly because 3rd grade word problems calculating area vs. perimeter seemed confusing. After such a quiz, my answer showed the fence yet again covered the entire area rather than going around it. So, my teacher sent me home with a note to Mom and Dad saying, ‘Chris killed the chickens again.’ For a farm kid that was unheard of! But I did learn soon after that it was meant in jest as the teacher was a farmer too.


Luckily for me, in the fifth grade, Mrs. Clark (of the Clark-Chapman House) managed to not only straighten out my abstract notions of math, but also instill an appreciation for all things historical, something that remains to this day.


My only broken bone ever is from falling off the monkey bars at William E. Norris school.


As a kid, you never appreciate how a dedicated teacher may influence your love of a particular subject. In my case, I have to truly thank my 8th grade French teacher, Mr. Paul Bassett. I had had an initial intro to language, with Mrs. Misner, but it was in 8th grade that the spark happened. I continued French through high school and then went on to be a French language major in college. Although my early dreams of being an interpreter at the United Nations didn't materialize, my background in French was responsible for my career path; first as a junior high teacher of English as a foreign language in French West Africa, and then, several more years working with village groups, understanding their perspective and priorities, and assisting them to achieve their community development goals. Languages can take you to magical places!


Do students learn how to diagram sentences anymore in English class? I fondly remember Mrs. Phillips trying to get us to understand sentence structure in 7th grade.


I graduated from Wm. E. Norris when the school itself was only 10 years old. I hope the doors we voted to replace/repair at the town meeting this week are not the originals!


Truth be told, I used to be a baton twirler leading the Memorial Day parade from Norris school to the center cemetery. No worries-you won't see that this year!
